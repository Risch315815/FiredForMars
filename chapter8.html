Chapter 8: Back Where I'm From

Changan grabbed Brendan's hand under their sheet.
"Just... try to not leave me."

Brendan let out a dry chuckle.
"Silly. Where am I supposed to go? The upper bunk?"
Changan paused and backed up a little bit, trying to see the whole picture.
"You don't sound like you're joking," Changan said. "You sound... stuck."
Brendan rolled his eyes.
"Duh. Aren't we all?"

Changan hesitated, then tried again.
"Yeah, but yours feels different. Like it goes deeper, more ancient. Not culturally, personally."
Brendan smiled, bitterly. He turned to face Changan, tapped the tip of his nose.
"It's cute when you try. Like a puppy trying to climb onto a sofa."
Then he rolled onto his back.
"I guess it's time. For you to meet my parents."

Changan held back a perfect roast and stayed neutral.
"Go on~"

Brendan's eyes drifted toward the ceiling.
"My parents had well-paid jobs, then AI replaced them. We ended up living on welfare."
He paused.
"They hated, no, abhor technology in general after that. Our farm became a bunker with no devices or terminals, nothing."
His voice dropped.
"I thought we were just poor, but when I tried to show my dad the old robot I spent six months trying to fix, he smashed it. With a shovel. In my face. Then walked away."

Changan couldn't hold in the roast any longer. "Meet your parents? What? Are you trying to marry~ me~?"

He expected Brendan to laugh, at least sarcastically, then tease him back, but Brendan didn't laugh.
He looked into Changan's eyes, with the rage of being disrespected. "You know what?" But then there was the disgust for Changan's lack of sensitivity. "Never mind." Finally just exhaustion. "Forget it."

Changan stiffened. Changan knew he had misread the moment, but he felt entitled to Brendan's tolerance for the inconvenience Brendan caused him, so he didn't apologize.
Instead, he pivoted.
"I wasn't smart as you, so I had to compete with tens of millions of people for a few thousand slots in the college entrance exam. I thought I missed a whole section in math, so I blamed myself for squandering my one and only chance to leave that broken mountain town. I pictured myself doing nothing but plowing the fields and selling empty plastic bottles for 2 whole months before the test result came out." He was bitter at first, but turned into bragging without noticing it. "Turned out I didn't miss the section, and I got into the top university. As I was supposed to."

Brendan felt heat rise in his chest.
"Hey. This is not a competition," He said. "But when I was in college, even the slightest hint of the inherent disadvantage of a female body because they have to offer a huge chunk of their energy to develop their womb, which they are probably never going to use, will get you cancelled indefinitely. That was what happened to my roommate, and he died in a gas station robbery when he was working the late night shift there."

Changan was radicalized. "How are there still gas stations? Where was he, Oklahoma?" He was incredulous. "Also, you think the chauvinist bashing was bad? Where I came from, if a man was seen kissing another man, both of them would be thrown into a bag and drown in a river."
Brendan was triggered. He turned on his back, started gasping for air; his eyes searched aimlessly, trying to hold onto a thought that can calm him down. Changan continued without noticing Brendan's panic attack: "After that, they would be hanged on the tree on the outskirt of the village, and their closest relatives would hang a board on them that condemns them for bringing disgrace to their family. Spoilers: it's usually by their parents."

Brendan, still trembling, could only utter: "I'm sorry to hear that."

Changan was caught in the heat of the moment, and didn't expect Brendan to back down.
"Y... Yeah."
Then he quickly realized how he was using a cruel gay-bashing tradition as a weapon, and felt immensely ashamed about himself. "Oh my god. I am so so sorry."
Then he noticed the horror on Brendan's face. "Are you okay?"

Brendan sighed. "I don't know. I don't think I'll ever get what I truly want. Maybe people like us will never, ever, get what we truly want."
Changan stayed quiet. Brendan's pinky brushed the back of his hand, tentative.
"I know it's selfish. I have more than most. But it still hurts."

There was a long silence between the two of them.

Changan broke the silence. "It doesn't have to be that way." He held Brendan's hand under their sheet again. "If you want us to meet your parents, we can."
Brendan smiled as if seeing the endless Kensas field again. "Yeah. We should go to Kensas. We can just walk along the Kensas river and talk for hours on end. We can have a small farm where we can grow our own food, raise our own cow and chicken, just the two of us. Just like my mom and dad."
Brendan felt the warmth in Changan's hand. "They would see how gentle and patient you are, and they would... want you to stay."
Changan turned towards Brendan, holding his face, thumb brushing through his stubble. "Is it okay for men to kiss in Mississippi?"
Brendan smiled. "Kensas, dummy."

Then he kissed him.
